# Sales benchmark library - competitor-page-audit

A vetted set of research-backed figures the skill may cite when framing the sales
impact of a finding. The rule: only cite from this file, or from a source you have
freshly validated the same way. Never invent a conversion number. Every entry below was
URL-validated (HTTP 200, real article content confirmed, not a soft-404) on 04-07-2026.

When you cite one, name the source in the output and link it. Re-validate any link older
than a few months before reusing it, and add new entries here only after the same check.

## Reviews and social proof

- A product with **five reviews is 270% more likely to be purchased** than a product
  with no reviews. The lift is **+190% for lower-priced products and +380% for
  higher-priced products**. Purchase likelihood **peaks at 4.0-4.7 stars** and dips as it
  approaches a suspiciously perfect 5.0. A **verified-buyer badge lifts purchase odds ~15%**.
  Source: Medill Spiegel Research Center, Northwestern University.
  https://spiegel.medill.northwestern.edu/how-online-reviews-influence-sales/

  Use for: any finding about missing or weak reviews/ratings. The price split matters -
  cite +380% only when the product is a considered/higher-priced purchase.

## Cart and page abandonment

- The **average documented online cart abandonment rate is 70.22%**, averaged across 50
  studies. Source: Baymard Institute.
  https://baymard.com/lists/cart-abandonment-rate

  Use for: framing why removing friction and doubt on the page matters - most sessions
  already leak before purchase, so the page has little margin to add hesitation.

## Product page UX quality

- Baymard's large-scale product-page usability research finds the **majority of
  e-commerce product pages carry significant UX problems** (their benchmark study reports
  most sites have "severe" or "mediocre" product-page UX). Source: Baymard Institute.
  https://baymard.com/blog/product-page-usability-report-and-benchmark

  Use for: framing that fixing PDP fundamentals is a common, high-leverage lever, and that
  template-level fixes compound across the catalogue.

## How to turn a benchmark into a claim (without overclaiming)

- These are **industry benchmarks, not the client's numbers**. Say so. The honest chain is:
  "research shows X drives Y; your page is missing X; therefore this is a credible lift to
  test." The test plan (see the skill's experiment step) is what converts a benchmark into
  a defensible, client-specific number.
- Prefer a **directional band tied to the mechanism** over a false-precise single number.
  "Reviews are one of the strongest documented conversion levers" plus the cited figure is
  stronger and safer than asserting "this will add 12% revenue."
