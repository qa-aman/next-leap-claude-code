# Scoring rubric - competitor-page-audit

Score each page 1-5 on every lens, using these written definitions. The point of a
fixed rubric is repeatability: two runs of the same two pages should land on similar
scores instead of drifting on gut feel. Anchor each score to the definition, not to a
comparison between the two pages (the gap comes later, from the difference of two
independently anchored scores).

General scale meaning, before the per-lens detail:

- **1 - Broken.** Actively works against the user. A reasonable visitor is blocked, misled, or lost.
- **2 - Poor.** Present but weak. The user gets through with effort and confusion.
- **3 - Adequate.** Does the job. Nothing is broken, nothing stands out.
- **4 - Strong.** Clearly better than adequate. Deliberate, considered, mostly frictionless.
- **5 - Best in class.** A visitor would notice and remember it. Hard to improve without diminishing returns.

---

## Lens 1 - Usability / UX flow

Judged by attempting the persona's goal step by step. Score what the walkthrough revealed.

- **1** - The persona cannot complete the goal, or hits a dead end with no recovery.
- **2** - The persona completes the goal but stalls or backtracks repeatedly; key next actions are unclear.
- **3** - The persona completes the goal with one or two moments of hesitation. No dead ends.
- **4** - The persona completes the goal smoothly. Next actions are obvious at each step; minor polish gaps only.
- **5** - The path is effortless and anticipatory: the page surfaces the right next action before the persona looks for it. No hesitation.

Screenshot-only note: if you cannot walk the flow, score only what a static frame shows about affordances and mark the lens downgraded. Do not award 4-5 from an image alone.

---

## Lens 2 - Feature / attribute parity

Judged by the presence and clarity of the elements and capabilities a visitor with this goal would expect.

- **1** - Missing several elements the goal requires; the page cannot support the job.
- **2** - Has the basics but lacks multiple expected elements (e.g. no search, no filters, no social proof, no pricing clarity).
- **3** - Has the expected elements for the goal, though some are thin or hard to find.
- **4** - Has the expected elements plus one or two differentiators that help the goal.
- **5** - Complete set of expected elements plus clear differentiators, each easy to find and well executed.

For the matrix, list each element as present/absent/partial on both pages so the gap is legible.

---

## Lens 3 - Messaging & copy

Judged by whether the page communicates value in the visitor's language, fast.

- **1** - Value is unclear after reading the first screen. Jargon, vague claims, or no clear "what is this".
- **2** - Value is discernible but buried, generic, or feature-led rather than benefit-led.
- **3** - Clear value proposition and CTA. Understandable, if unremarkable.
- **4** - Sharp, benefit-led headline; CTA wording is specific; microcopy reduces doubt; some credible proof.
- **5** - The copy names the visitor's problem in their own words, the CTA is compelling and specific, and proof (numbers, names, quotes) is concrete and believable.

---

## Lens 4 - Visual & conversion design

Judged by how the layout directs attention toward the goal and the primary action.

- **1** - No clear hierarchy; the primary action is hard to spot or competes with clutter.
- **2** - Hierarchy exists but is muddled; CTA is present but not prominent; weak or absent trust signals.
- **3** - Clear hierarchy, a visible primary CTA, adequate whitespace and trust signals above the fold.
- **4** - Deliberate hierarchy that leads the eye to the CTA; strong trust signals; above-the-fold content earns the scroll.
- **5** - Every visual choice serves the conversion: the eye lands on the primary action first, trust is established immediately, nothing distracts.
