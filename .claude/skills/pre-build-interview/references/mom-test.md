# Mom Test - Question Design Rules

Source: Rob Fitzpatrick, *The Mom Test*.

The core insight: people lie when asked about hypotheticals, opinions, and the future. They tell the truth when asked about their past, their specific actions, and the costs they have already paid.

## The three rules

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea.**
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not generics or opinions about the future.**
3. **Talk less, listen more.**

## Bad question patterns and rewrites

Every question in the interview guide must be checked against this table.

| Bad question | Why it fails | Rewrite |
|---|---|---|
| "Would you use a tool that does X?" | Hypothetical, asks for opinion, pitches the feature | "Tell me about the last time you tried to do X. What did you use?" |
| "Do you think X is a problem?" | Asks for opinion. Everyone says yes to be polite. | "Walk me through the last time X happened. What did you do?" |
| "How much would you pay for X?" | Future commitment with no cost. Numbers will be inflated. | "What have you spent on solving X so far? Tools, time, anything." |
| "How often do you have this problem?" | Generic. Answers will be vague ("often", "sometimes"). | "When was the last time it happened? And the time before that?" |
| "Would this be useful to you?" | Pitch disguised as a question. Compliance bias. | (Do not ask. Cut this question.) |
| "How frustrating is it when X breaks?" | Leading. Embeds the answer in the question. | "What happened the last time X broke? What did you do next?" |
| "If we built X, would you switch?" | Pure hypothetical. The honest answer is "I do not know". | "Tell me about the last time you switched from one tool to another. What pushed you to do it?" |

## The compliment trap

When users compliment the idea ("that sounds great", "you should build this"), it is meaningless. Compliments are the warning sign that the interview has gone off the rails. Do not write them in the notes. Redirect:

- "What would you have to see to actually use this?"
- "What is keeping you on your current solution?"
- "Who else needs to be involved for you to switch?"

## The commitment ladder

Real interest shows up as commitment, not words. Each rung is stronger than words alone:

1. They name a specific past incident.
2. They show you their current workaround on the call.
3. They introduce you to someone else who has the problem.
4. They agree to a follow-up call.
5. They put money or time on the line (deposit, beta access fee, calendar time).

If by the end of the interview you have not climbed at least to rung 2, the signal is weak no matter what they said.

## Live discipline during the interview

- Ask the question. Then stop talking. Count to 5 in your head. The second answer is usually the real one.
- When they generalize, anchor back: "When was the last time?"
- When they say "always" or "never", ask for the most recent counterexample.
- When they pitch back at you ("you should add X"), ask "why" until you find the underlying problem they are solving.
- Note emotional signals: sighs, laughs, "ugh", "honestly". These are stronger than stated opinions.

## Red flags in your own behavior

If you catch yourself doing any of these mid-interview, stop and reset:

- Explaining the feature
- Defending the current product
- Correcting their understanding
- Saying "what if we..."
- Filling silence with another question before they answer the first
